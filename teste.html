Coffee grains are the best snack ever made

I know, I know—this might sound like an unpopular opinion, but hear me out. The idea of munching on coffee grains as a snack might raise a few eyebrows, but it's a notion worth exploring. Curiosity has always been a driving force for humanity, leading us to discover new tastes and experiences. It was this same curiosity that led me to pop a coffee grain into my mouth for the first time. The result? A delightful revelation that I believe deserves more attention.

Brazil and Coffee (Every family has its own Rory and Lorelai)

Living in Brazil, coffee isn't just a beverage; it's a cultural staple. As the world's largest producer of coffee, Brazil contributes approximately one-third of global coffee production. This deep-rooted connection to coffee permeates our daily lives, much like the Gilmore Girls' infamous coffee addiction. In many Brazilian households, it's not uncommon to enjoy a cup of coffee late into the evening. Over time, this habitual consumption has desensitized many of us to its stimulating effects, making coffee a comforting constant rather than just a morning pick-me-up.

The diet bitter overexpensive snack of the week

Now, let's talk about coffee grains as a snack. At first glance, it might seem unconventional, but consider this: coffee beans are low in calories and packed with antioxidants. A 50-gram serving contains about 260 calories. That means each individual coffee bean has roughly 5.2 calories.

Comparatively, many low-calorie snacks on the market are both expensive and less satisfying. Imagine purchasing a trendy health snack that costs a small fortune and leaves you hungry an hour later. Now, consider a serving of 30 coffee grains. At approximately 5.2 calories per bean, that's a mere 156 calories. If we estimate the cost of coffee beans at $0.12 per gram, and with each bean weighing about 1 gram, a 30-bean snack would cost around $3.60.

Sure, it's a bit on the pricey side for a snack, but isn't that the hallmark of the latest fitness trends? Overpriced, slightly bitter, and touted by influencers as the next big thing. So, here's my (sarcastic) prediction: soon, we'll see fitness enthusiasts swapping their kale chips for artisanal, hand-roasted coffee bean snacks. After all, nothing says "health-conscious" like chewing on the source of your morning brew.

In conclusion, while it might not be for everyone, snacking on coffee grains offers a unique combination of flavor, cultural homage, and a nod to the ever-evolving landscape of health trends. Give it a try—you might just find yourself reaching for a handful of beans instead of that energy bar.

Ghibli inspired AI Art is TRASH

Seemingly overnight, the internet has been flooded with AI-generated images mimicking the enchanting style of Studio Ghibli. Tools like ChatGPT and DALL-E have made it effortless for anyone to transform photos or prompts into Ghibli-esque artworks. While many casual observers find this trend harmless and amusing, a significant portion of the artistic community has voiced strong opposition. This raises important questions about the role of artificial intelligence in art, especially when it encroaches upon the creative domains of established artists. While AI-generated art can be intriguing and innovative, it crosses a line when it infringes upon the rights and styles of living artists. In the case of Hayao Miyazaki, the mastermind behind Studio Ghibli, his explicit disapproval of AI art suggests that, despite the technological capability to replicate his style, we should refrain from doing so out of respect for his artistic integrity.

On Hayao Miyazaki

Hayao Miyazaki, born in 1941, co-founded Studio Ghibli in 1985 and has since become a monumental figure in the world of animation. His films, such as My Neighbor Totoro, Spirited Away, and Princess Mononoke, are celebrated for their hand-drawn artistry, complex characters, and profound narratives that often explore themes of nature, pacifism, and humanism. Miyazaki's dedication to traditional animation techniques is not merely a stylistic choice but a reflection of his belief in the transformative power of art. He views the painstaking process of creating animation by hand as integral to the development of an artist's character and worldview. This philosophy underscores his conviction that the essence of art lies in the human experience and the emotions imbued by the creator. Artificial intelligence, in his view, lacks the capacity to understand or replicate this depth of human sentiment. In a 2016 documentary, upon being shown AI-generated animation, Miyazaki remarked that it was "an insult to life itself," expressing profound discomfort with the notion of removing human intention and emotion from the creative process.

Why it is disrespectful

The replication of an artist's unique style through artificial means raises significant ethical and philosophical concerns. Art is not merely about the end product but encompasses the journey of creation—the struggles, insights, and personal growth that the artist experiences. By using AI to mimic the distinctive style of a creator like Miyazaki, we risk trivializing the years of dedication and the deeply personal process that contribute to such artistry. This act can be seen as a form of intellectual theft, stripping the original work of its context and meaning. Moreover, it undermines the value of human creativity, suggesting that the nuances of an artist's vision can be reduced to algorithms and data sets. Respecting Miyazaki's stance on AI art means acknowledging the irreplaceable nature of human touch in creative endeavors. Instead of seeking shortcuts through technology to replicate his life's work, aspiring artists should engage with the medium directly, embracing the challenges and rewards that come with genuine creation. This approach not only honors the legacy of artists like Miyazaki but also fosters originality and personal expression in art.

In conclusion, while AI technology offers fascinating possibilities, its application in replicating the unique styles of living artists without consent is both disrespectful and ethically questionable. Hayao Miyazaki's clear opposition to AI-generated art serves as a poignant reminder of the importance of preserving the human essence in creative works. As consumers and creators, we have a responsibility to respect the boundaries set by artists and to value the irreplaceable qualities that human creativity brings to art.
